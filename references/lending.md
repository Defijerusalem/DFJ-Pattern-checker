## Global evidence-quality standard (applies to every pattern below)

A clean NOT PRESENT requires a specific, named, independently-checkable fact — actual on-chain collateral composition data, a named oracle provider with disclosed configuration, a documented isolation-mode/cap setting. Vendor language ("robust risk management," "carefully vetted collateral," "industry-leading risk framework") is never sufficient. When only this kind of language is available, output SIMILAR MATCH with a note that primary-source (on-chain or governance-forum) verification is needed.

## Before applying any pattern below: check protocol architecture

Not every lending protocol has a single, shared collateral pool. Some (e.g., Morpho Blue, and similar permissionless market-creation frameworks) let anyone deploy isolated markets, each with its own independently-chosen collateral asset, oracle, and risk parameters — and risk in one market cannot spread to another by design. For this architecture type:
- Pattern 1 (Collateral Concentration) must be applied **per individual market**, not as a protocol-wide aggregate. A statement like "Protocol X is 40% concentrated in Asset Y" is meaningless if X is actually 180 independent markets — instead, identify and score the specific market in question.
- If the user's request is about "the protocol" generally rather than a named market, say so explicitly and ask which market, or note that a protocol-wide score is not meaningful for this architecture type rather than producing a falsely aggregated number.
- For single-pool protocols (e.g., Aave, Compound), Pattern 1 applies at the protocol level as originally designed below.

## Pattern 1: Collateral Concentration in Cross-Chain-Dependent Assets

**What to check:** what percentage of the protocol's total collateral base is composed of a single non-stablecoin asset, and whether that asset's backing depends on a cross-chain bridge the lending protocol itself does not control.

**Why this matters — real case:**
- Aave accepted rsETH (KelpDAO's liquid restaking token) as collateral, with concentrated e-mode leverage. rsETH's backing depended on a LayerZero bridge route configured as 1-of-1 (see `bridge.md` Pattern 1). When that bridge was exploited, 116,500 unbacked rsETH entered circulation and was used as collateral on Aave — Aave's own contracts were never touched, but it absorbed $6.6B in TVL impact and $123.7M-$230.1M in bad debt.

**How to check:**
- Pull on-chain collateral composition (subgraph query, DeFiLlama, or the protocol's own risk dashboard if published).
- Flag any single non-stablecoin asset representing >25% of total collateral value.
- For any flagged asset, trace its backing mechanism — is it a liquid staking/restaking token, a bridged/wrapped asset? If yes, check that asset's own dependency chain against `bridge.md`.

**Match criteria:**
- EXACT MATCH: a cross-chain-dependent asset (LST, LRT, wrapped token) represents >25% of collateral AND its backing bridge has an unresolved Bridge-category EXACT MATCH finding.
- SIMILAR MATCH: concentration exists but the underlying bridge dependency is undisclosed or unverified.
- NOT PRESENT: collateral base is diversified (no single non-stablecoin asset >25%) or concentrated assets have verified, independently-secured backing.

## Pattern 2: Exotic/Non-Standard Token Integration Risk

**What to check:** whether any listed collateral asset uses a non-standard token implementation with transfer hooks, callbacks, or donation-manipulable accounting — properties that can be weaponized even though the token "works as designed." *(Note: the specific standards named below — ERC-777, ERC-20 — are Ethereum/EVM-specific. On other chains, check for the equivalent concept using that chain's actual token standard: e.g., a Solana SPL token with a non-standard transfer hook extension, or a Move-based token with custom transfer logic. The underlying risk — a token whose transfer behavior does something unexpected beyond a simple balance change — applies on any chain; the specific standard name does not.)*

**Why this matters — real case:**
- Cream Finance listed AMP, an ERC-777 token, as collateral on Feb 10, 2021. ERC-777's transfer-callback feature created a reentrancy surface that didn't exist in standard ERC-20 tokens. The token worked exactly as designed — the risk was Cream's choice to accept it as collateral without accounting for that design difference. Exploited for $34M six months after listing.
- Separately, Cream's October 2021 exploit ($130M+) involved a Yearn vault token (crYUSD) whose exchange rate could be manipulated by donating assets directly to the underlying vault — a donation-manipulable share-price pattern, distinct from the ERC-777 issue but same root category: accepting a collateral asset whose accounting can be externally manipulated.
- Resupply (June 26, 2025, $9.5M): a third, independent confirmation of the exact donation-manipulation mechanism, four years after Cream. The attacker targeted a newly deployed ERC-4626 vault (crvUSD/wstUSR market) just 1.5 hours after it went live, while liquidity was still near zero. A flash loan funded a small donation plus a 1-wei deposit; the resulting exchange-rate calculation (1e36 divided by an artificially inflated price) floor-divided to zero, letting the attacker borrow the protocol's entire $10M debt limit against 1 wei of collateral. The specific new signal this case adds: newly deployed, low-liquidity vaults are measurably higher risk in the window immediately after launch, before organic liquidity accumulates — the same donation-manipulation mechanism is easier to execute cheaply against an empty vault than an established one.
- Raft Protocol (November 10, 2023, $6.7M in unbacked R stablecoin minted, ~$1.3-3.6M net realized loss depending on source — the attacker accidentally burned most of the stolen ETH to a null address, resulting in a net loss for the attacker themselves) — a genuinely distinct variant demonstrating that donation-inflated index/exchange-rate calculations can be exploited via rounding in *either* direction, not only the floor-division-to-zero pattern above. The attacker donated collateral directly to the `InterestRatePositionManager` contract, then liquidated a pre-existing position, inflating the contract's internal index value by over 1000x. This let a `divUp()` function — designed to round *up* rather than down, guaranteeing a minimum return of 1 for any non-zero input regardless of how small — mint exactly 1 wei of a share token for 1 wei of real collateral, repeated 60 times. Because the index had been inflated, redeeming those 60 wei of shares returned thousands of times more real collateral than was ever deposited. Both Raft's contracts had been audited by two separate, reputable firms (Trail of Bits and Hats Finance) before the exploit. The lesson distinct from Resupply's case: check rounding behavior in *both* directions — a function using round-up-to-minimum semantics to protect against zero-value edge cases can itself become the exploit vector when combined with an externally inflatable index, just as a round-down-to-zero function can when combined with the same donation.
- Sonne Finance (May 14-15, 2024, ~$20M, Optimism deployment only — the Base deployment was unaffected): a fourth, independent, and unusually well-documented confirmation, this time as part of a named, recurring vulnerability class specific to Compound V2 forks. The protocol added a new VELO market via governance, splitting the required setup into two separately-timelocked transactions (market creation, then collateral-factor activation) — both individually permissionless to execute once their timelocks expired. The attacker executed both the moment each timelock cleared, then flash-loaned VELO tokens and donated them directly to the freshly created, still-empty soVELO market, inflating its exchange rate before the protocol's own team could seed it with a protective minimum deposit (the same mitigation Resupply's case above independently confirms). Multiple independent sources explicitly name this as "a well-known issue... exploited multiple times in the past, including Hundred Finance and Onyx Protocol" (and separately, Midas Capital) — meaning Sonne is not an isolated case but a documented, recurring pattern specific to an entire lineage of forked lending protocols that inherited the same unmitigated donation surface from Compound V2's original codebase.

**Move-chain translation note (added after Aave/Aptos dry run):** Move's resource model (used by Sui, Aptos, and other Move-based chains) treats assets as linear resources that cannot be implicitly duplicated or "double-counted" — collateral cannot exist in two places at once by construction, a property explicitly cited as the structural reason Euler's $80M reuse-of-collateral mechanism could not occur the same way in Move. This does not mean Pattern 2 cannot manifest on a Move-based chain at all — a real Move exploit exists (Typus protocol, whitelist-validation/oracle-price case, October 2025), confirming that language-level resource safety does not replace audits for logic outside the resource model itself, such as price oracle access control. But the specific mechanism Pattern 2 was built to catch (donation-inflated `balanceOf()`-style accounting) is structurally harder to construct in Move than in Solidity. A Move-based protocol should not be assumed guilty by EVM-pattern default, nor cleared by language alone without checking the actual calculation.

**How to check:**
- For each collateral asset, check the token standard. Flag any non-standard implementation (ERC-777, rebasing tokens, tokens with transfer fees/hooks).
- Check whether any collateral asset's exchange rate or share price is calculated from a pool/vault balance that can be altered by direct donation (i.e., sending tokens to the contract without going through the official deposit function).
- Check the vault's deployment age. A newly deployed vault or market (hours to days old) with low liquidity is a materially higher-risk window for this exact mechanism — Resupply's vault was exploited 1.5 hours after launch. Flag any collateral market that accepts deposits before a minimum liquidity floor or seed deposit has been established.
- For Compound V2-derived lending protocols (forks) specifically: check whether a new market's activation is split across multiple separately-timelocked governance transactions (e.g., market creation, then collateral-factor activation as separate steps). If each step is individually permissionless once its own timelock expires, an attacker can execute the exact moment a step clears and act before the protocol team can seed the market — Sonne Finance, Hundred Finance, Onyx Protocol, and Midas Capital all lost funds to this same mechanism.
- If a protocol has already implemented a donation-limit safeguard (a maximum single-donation size relative to pool balance), check whether that limit can be circumvented by repeating many donations below the threshold rather than one large donation above it. Wise Lending (January 12, 2024, ~$464K) had exactly this protection in place — a single donation could not exceed a set percentage of the pool — but the attacker donated repeatedly in amounts just under that limit while cycling deposits and withdrawals, accumulating the same rounding-error advantage the safeguard was designed to prevent, just spread across many small, individually-compliant transactions instead of one large one.

**Cross-reference note:** the donation-manipulable share-price half of this pattern (Cream's October 2021 case) is typically funded by a flash loan in practice — a large, momentary balance is enough to distort the exchange rate within a single transaction. This pattern already fully covers that mechanism; do not score flash-loan funding as a separate, additional finding when it's the funding method behind an otherwise-already-flagged donation-manipulation finding.

**Match criteria:**
- EXACT MATCH: collateral asset uses ERC-777 or equivalent callback-enabled standard with no documented mitigation, OR exchange rate is calculated from a directly-donatable balance.
- SIMILAR MATCH: non-standard token present but documented mitigations exist (e.g., reentrancy guards, donation-resistant accounting) — verify mitigation is real, not just claimed.
- NOT PRESENT: all collateral assets are standard ERC-20 with accounting derived from internally-tracked balances, not raw `balanceOf()` reads.

## Pattern 3: Curator/Allocator Risk

**What to check:** for vault-based or curated lending products (where a third party decides which underlying markets a depositor's funds flow into, rather than the depositor choosing a specific market directly), whether the curator's allocation strategy and track record are transparent and verifiable.

**Why this matters — real cases, independently confirmed twice:**
- Stream Finance xUSD collapse (November 2025): multiple Morpho/Euler curators (TelosC, MEV Capital, Re7 Labs, and others) allocated depositor funds into markets accepting xUSD as collateral, chasing its high yield. Public warnings about xUSD's transparency and leverage risk were raised days before the collapse and reportedly disregarded. When xUSD depegged from $1 to $0.33, the resulting bad debt (~$285M exposure across the ecosystem; ~$27.4M at Re7 Labs alone) accrued directly to depositors in the affected vaults — isolated to those specific vaults, but a real, realized loss from a curator's allocation choice, not a contract bug.
- Resolv USR exploit (March 2026): at least 15 Morpho vaults curated by Gauntlet, Re7 Labs, kpk, and 9summits had allocated depositor funds into markets accepting USR as collateral. When USR was exploited, multiple curators had to open after-the-fact compensation pools (Gauntlet: $4.37M; Re7: $223K) — a second, independent instance of the same mechanism: a curator's allocation decision, not Morpho Blue's own contract code, causing the loss.
- Both cases confirm the same structural point: Morpho Blue's isolated-market design contained the damage to the specific affected vaults rather than socializing it protocol-wide — the architecture worked as intended. The loss was a curator judgment failure, not a platform failure, and depositors in those specific curated vaults bore it directly.

**How to check:**
- Identify whether the product in question is a direct market position (depositor chooses the specific market) or a curated vault (a third party allocates across markets on the depositor's behalf).
- For curated vaults, check whether the curator publishes allocation strategy, track record, and the specific underlying markets currently in use.
- Check whether the curator has any disclosed history of allocating into a market that was later exploited or whose collateral depegged — both validating cases above are now searchable, named incidents.
- Cross-check the underlying markets a vault allocates to against Patterns 1 and 2 above — curator risk often manifests as "good vault interface, risky underlying markets," so the underlying market check still matters even when a curator layer exists.

**Match criteria:**
- EXACT MATCH: curator has a confirmed, named, dated history of allocating depositor funds into a market that was later exploited or whose collateral depegged with resulting bad debt (e.g., Stream Finance xUSD, Resolv USR), with no documented change to allocation due-diligence process since.
- SIMILAR MATCH: curator allocations are opaque, undisclosed, or the underlying markets fail Pattern 1/2 checks, but no confirmed realized-loss incident is found for this specific curator.
- NOT PRESENT: curator publishes transparent, verifiable allocation strategy, underlying markets independently pass Pattern 1/2 checks, and no confirmed realized-loss incident is found for this specific curator.

## Pattern 4 (WATCH-ITEM — single incident, not yet two-case validated): Oracle Composition Error (Partial Price Feed Update)

**What to check:** whether a collateral asset's price is derived from a multi-leg calculation (e.g., assetX/ETH combined with ETH/USD to produce assetX/USD), and whether a governance-approved update to one leg of that calculation was verified to correctly update every leg, not just the one being changed.

**Confidence note:** this pattern currently rests on a single named incident. Per this methodology's standing rule, a pattern needs two independent, real, dollar-quantified cases sharing the same mechanism before it can reach EXACT MATCH status. Until a second case is found, treat findings here as SIMILAR MATCH ceiling, not EXACT MATCH — the mechanism is real and worth checking, but not yet validated at full confidence.

**Why this is flagged — real case:**
- Moonwell (Base/Optimism, February 2026, $1.8M): a governance-approved update (MIP-X43) intended to adjust cbETH pricing linked the cbETH/USD price feed only to the cbETH/ETH leg, omitting the ETH/USD component entirely. This mispriced cbETH at approximately $1.12 instead of its real value of roughly $2,200 — a multi-thousand-fold error. Automated liquidation bots, which do not sanity-check oracle outputs against recent price history, immediately began liquidating positions at the incorrect rate. This is distinct from Pattern 1 (cross-chain collateral concentration) and distinct from a single-source manipulable feed (the Derivatives Pattern 2 mechanism) — the oracle here was not manipulated by an attacker at all; it was a self-inflicted configuration error introduced through the protocol's own governance process, where a partial update to a multi-leg price calculation was approved and deployed without verifying the full calculation chain remained intact.

**How to check:**
- For each collateral asset priced via a multi-leg or composite calculation, list every leg required to produce the final USD (or reference-currency) price.
- Check whether recent governance proposals or protocol updates modified pricing configuration for any collateral asset. If so, verify the update changed or was tested against every leg of the calculation, not only the leg explicitly named in the proposal.
- Check whether any automated sanity check exists comparing a new oracle price against a recent historical range before it is accepted as valid for liquidation purposes — a circuit breaker independent of the governance/update process itself.

**Match criteria:**
- SIMILAR MATCH (ceiling for this pattern currently): collateral is priced via a multi-leg calculation and no sanity-check/circuit-breaker mechanism is confirmed to exist independent of the update process. This is the maximum reachable status until a second validating incident is found.
- NOT PRESENT: collateral pricing is single-leg (no composite calculation to get wrong), or a confirmed, independent sanity-check mechanism exists that would catch a multi-thousand-fold pricing error before it reaches the liquidation path.

## Concentrated Control Risk (cross-cutting — see SKILL.md)

The question "how many independent parties have to collude to take a severe, unilateral action" is no longer a lending-specific pattern. It generalized across lending governance (Solend's SLND1 fund-seizure vote), bridge admin multisigs (Frax's 3-of-5 team control), and base-layer validator/consensus concentration (Hyperliquid's core-contributor token concentration) — the same underlying question, appearing at different layers. This check now lives as a cross-cutting rule in SKILL.md, applied alongside whichever category-specific file(s) are in use, rather than being a lending-only pattern. See SKILL.md's "Concentrated Control Risk" section for the full check; Solend's SLND1 incident remains the validating case.

## Pattern 5: Unvalidated External Contract Address Parameters in Leverage/Composability Functions

**What to check:** whether a periphery or leverage/deleverage function accepts a caller-supplied contract address (a "market," "pool," or similar reference) as a parameter and executes calls against it without verifying that address corresponds to a genuine, whitelisted instance of the expected contract type.

**Why this matters — two independent, realized, dollar-quantified cases confirming the same mechanism, further reinforced by a named industry-wide trend:**
- Exactly Protocol (August 18, 2023, ~$7.2-7.6M, Optimism): the `DebtManager` periphery contract's `leverage()` function accepted a `Market` address parameter with no validation that it referenced a genuine, whitelisted market. The attacker deployed a fake contract mimicking a legitimate market's interface, passed its address into `leverage()` alongside a forged permit, and had the real `DebtManager` execute a deposit call against the fake contract. Inside that fake contract's `deposit()` function, the attacker re-entered `DebtManager.crossDeleverage()`, this time targeting the real USDC market, and drained collateral by manipulating account health factors before performing partial liquidations to extract further value. A copycat attacker replicated the exact technique roughly three hours later for an additional $260K, confirming the exploit was straightforward to reproduce once the technique was public.
- LI.FI (2024, contributing to a documented $21M+ across 18 similar incidents that year): a newly deployed `GasZipFacet` contract, part of a multi-chain liquidity aggregation protocol, lacked adequate validation on external contract calls. The attacker deployed a malicious contract and used it to process arbitrary external transactions, triggering unauthorized `transferFrom` operations against users who had granted the protocol standing approvals — mechanistically the same "unvalidated external call target" root cause as Exactly, in a different product type. Three Sigma's own 2024 exploit analysis names "arbitrary external calls" as a distinct, named, recognized attack vector responsible for this class of loss across the year, not a one-off implementation mistake specific to either protocol.


**How this differs from Pattern 2 (Exotic Token Integration) and DEX/AMM Pattern 3 (Unrestricted Call-Target Validation):** Pattern 2 is about a listed *collateral token's* own transfer-hook behavior being weaponized. DEX/AMM Pattern 3 is about a *swap aggregator* letting a caller supply an arbitrary executor address for a trade. This pattern is distinct from both — it is specifically about a *lending/leverage periphery function* trusting a caller-supplied reference to another *contract* (not a token, not a swap target) as if it were guaranteed to be a genuine instance of the protocol's own market/pool type, when it can instead be an attacker's fabricated substitute.

**How to check:**
- Identify any function that accepts a contract address as a parameter and subsequently calls into that address expecting a specific interface or behavior (e.g., "market," "pool," "vault" parameters in leverage, deleverage, or migration functions).
- Confirm whether that address is checked against an on-chain whitelist or registry of known-legitimate contracts before being used, or whether it is trusted implicitly based only on interface compliance (which a malicious contract can trivially mimic).
- Check whether the function that receives the untrusted address makes any external call back out (e.g., a `deposit()` call) before completing its own state changes — this is what creates the reentrancy opportunity once a fake contract is substituted in.

**Match criteria:**
- EXACT MATCH: a periphery/leverage function accepts a caller-supplied contract address for a critical parameter (market, pool, vault) with no on-chain whitelist check, and subsequently makes an external call to that address before completing its own state updates.
- SIMILAR MATCH: a whitelist check exists but its completeness or enforcement is not independently verifiable, or the external call ordering relative to state updates is unclear from available documentation.
- NOT PRESENT: all contract address parameters in leverage/composability functions are confirmed validated against an on-chain whitelist before use, or the function follows checks-effects-interactions ordering strictly enough that a substituted fake contract could not extract value even if accepted.

## Pattern 6: Compound-Fork Empty/Near-Empty Market Exchange-Rate Donation Inflation

**What to check:** whether a Compound-v2-style money market (or a fork of one) computes its share-to-underlying exchange rate as a live ratio of `(cash + totalBorrows − totalReserves) / totalSupply`, and whether that market can reach a state where `totalSupply` is zero or near-zero while still accepting direct token donations to its `cash` balance.

**Why this matters — real cases:** this is the lending-specific variant of the donation-manipulable share-price mechanism already validated generally in `yield-aggregator.md` Pattern 2 (GoGoPool/Thetanuts), but it recurs so densely and specifically across Compound-v2 forks that it deserves its own explicit entry here — at least 10 independent, dollar-quantified cases follow the identical shape:
- Sonne Finance (~$20M total across the campaign; this specific mechanism's single largest reproduced transaction ~$724K) and Hundred Finance (two separate incidents, ~$7.4M reported on the second) — both classic empty-cToken-market donation attacks on Optimism.
- Onyx Protocol (two separate incidents, ~$3.8M and ~$2M) and 0VIX (~$2.0M) — same mechanism on different Compound forks.
- Radiant Capital (~$4.5M) and Wise Lending (two related incidents, ~$464K and a separate ~$260K rescued by a whitehat before exploitation) — same root cause expressed through a RAY-scaled liquidity index rather than the classic `exchangeRate` formula, confirming the mechanism generalizes beyond the literal Compound v2 code shape to any share-index accounting following the same "empty pool + live donation" precondition.
- bZx/Fulcrum (~$208K) — an early (2020) confirming case, establishing this is not a recently-discovered mechanism but a persistent, still-recurring one across new forks years later.

**How to check:**
- Identify every market/pool in the protocol and confirm whether any can realistically reach (or start at) `totalSupply == 0` or a very small `totalSupply` while still being open to deposits — a brand-new market immediately after listing is the highest-risk moment.
- Check whether the exchange-rate/index calculation reads the token's raw `balanceOf(this)` (or an equivalent live balance) as part of `cash`, meaning a direct, non-`mint()` token transfer to the market contract changes the computed rate.
- Check for a minimum-liquidity/seed-deposit safeguard (a "dead" initial mint, a minimum `totalSupply` floor before the market accepts further activity, or a first-depositor-only privileged seeding step) — the specific mitigation already validated as effective is minting a small amount of permanently-locked "ghost" shares at market creation, which most of the cases above lacked.

**Match criteria:**
- EXACT MATCH: a money market's exchange-rate calculation is a live function of `balanceOf(this)`/cash, AND the market can reach a near-zero `totalSupply` state while still accepting external token transfers, with no minimum-liquidity safeguard.
- SIMILAR MATCH: a safeguard exists (minimum seed liquidity, ghost shares) but its sufficiency against a large, well-funded donation has not been independently verified.
- NOT PRESENT: the protocol confirms every market is seeded with a permanently-locked minimum liquidity floor at creation, or the exchange-rate calculation does not depend on a raw, donatable balance.

## Pattern 7: Read-Only Reentrancy on Balance-Derived LP/Vault Collateral Pricing

**What to check:** whether a lending protocol prices LP-token or vault-share collateral (from Curve, Balancer, or a similar AMM) by calling a balance-derived rate function (`get_virtual_price()`, a pool's balance/rate getter) during a window where the underlying pool's own liquidity-changing function has started sending tokens out but hasn't yet finished updating its internal accounting — a "read-only" reentrancy, since the view call itself never reverts, it just returns a transiently wrong number.

**Why this matters — real cases:** at least 6 independent, dollar-quantified cases share this exact mechanism:
- dForce (~$3.65M) — Curve `remove_liquidity` reentered into dForce's own price oracle reading a wstETH/CRV gauge token mid-call.
- Conic Finance's ETH Omnipool (~$3.25M) and Sturdy Finance (~$800K) — both priced Balancer/Curve LP collateral through a balance-based rate read during the same reentrancy window.
- Sentiment Protocol (~$1.0M) — Balancer BPT collateral priced the same way.
- Market.xyz/a Hundred Finance clone (~$180K) and Rari Capital's `ibETH`/Alpha Homora integration — smaller-scale confirming cases of the identical mechanism.

**How to check:**
- For any collateral type backed by an AMM LP token or a vault share, identify the specific function used to price it and confirm whether that function reads a live, balance-derived rate from the underlying pool (rather than a TWAP or an independent oracle).
- Check whether the underlying pool's own liquidity-removal function (`remove_liquidity`, `exitPool`, or equivalent) sends tokens to the caller before finalizing its internal balance/rate accounting — this is the precondition that makes the read-only window exploitable.
- Check whether the price-read function is protected by the *same* reentrancy lock as the pool's state-changing functions (a lock scoped only to the lending protocol's own functions does not help, since the reentrancy happens inside the AMM pool's own call stack) — Curve's newer pools expose a dedicated reentrancy-check function specifically so integrators can call it before trusting a balance-derived read; confirm whether the protocol actually uses it.

**Match criteria:**
- EXACT MATCH: collateral pricing reads a balance-derived rate from an external AMM pool with no confirmed reentrancy-safety check, AND the underlying pool's liquidity-removal function sends tokens before finalizing internal accounting.
- SIMILAR MATCH: the pricing source is balance-derived but reentrancy-safety of the specific read path is undocumented or unverified from available code/audits.
- NOT PRESENT: collateral pricing uses a TWAP or independent oracle rather than a live pool balance, or the protocol is confirmed to call the AMM's own reentrancy-check function (e.g., Curve's) before trusting the read.

## What this file does NOT cover

- Pure code-logic bugs unrelated to collateral choice (missing health checks, arithmetic errors in liquidation math) — these are Universal Core / audit-weight issues. The Euler Finance case (missing insolvency check in `donateToReserves`) is the reference negative result: six professional audits missed it, and no collateral-listing decision would have caught it either, because the flaw was in Euler's own code, not in what it chose to accept.
- A new security check added during a contract upgrade can be silently bypassed if the old, unprotected version of the contract remains live and shares underlying state (a fund pool, an accounting ledger) with the new version (one real case found: Pine Protocol, December 2023, ~$1.4M — an NFT-backed lending protocol upgraded its contracts to add a `whitelistedIntermediaries` check to its flash-loan function, a real security improvement. The old, pre-upgrade version of the contract, however, remained live at an address that shared the same fund pool as the new version, and had never enforced that check. The attacker borrowed against NFT collateral through the new, protected contract, then took a flash loan through the old, unprotected contract to repay the initial borrow — because both contracts drew from and repaid to the same shared pool, the repayment satisfied the flash loan's accounting regardless of which contract version's rules should have applied, letting the attacker repeat the cycle and drain the vault). This is distinct from a simple deprecated-and-forgotten contract carrying its own isolated bug (see the cross-reference to `yield-aggregator.md` above) — the specific risk here is that a genuine security upgrade can be rendered meaningless if the pre-upgrade path is not also disabled or migrated away from shared resources. **How to check:** when a protocol has upgraded its contracts to add a new security check, verify that the previous version of the contract is either fully disabled or does not share fund pools, accounting state, or balances with the new, protected version.
- Interest rate model design and liquidation incentive tuning — these are economic design choices, not pattern-matchable security risks in the same sense.
- Yield-tokenization/Principal Token (PT) pricing manipulation via low-liquidity trades (one real case found: Loopscale, April 26 2025, $5.8M — an attacker manipulated the price oracle for RateX PT collateral via a low-liquidity trade, enabling undercollateralized loans, just two weeks after the protocol's launch and despite a prior audit). This is mechanistically distinct from Pattern 1 (the asset itself was not bridge-compromised or unbacked) and from Pattern 2 (this is oracle/pricing-curve manipulation, not donation-manipulable balance accounting) — it sits closer to a lending-specific variant of oracle-manipulable exotic collateral pricing. Currently a single incident; searched for a second case sharing this specific PT/yield-token pricing mechanism and did not find one during this pass. Documented as a genuine, evidenced watch candidate, not yet promotable to a scored pattern under the two-incident rule.
- Manipulable internal wrapper/vault exchange-rate conversion for RWA-wrapped collateral, distinct from both Pattern 2 and Pattern 4 (one real case found: Edel Finance, July 2026, ~$403K — a lending protocol accepted wGOOGLx, a wrapped tokenized-equity share, as collateral, deriving its USD value as `ExternalPrice(GOOGLx) × InternalRate(wGOOGLx/GOOGLx)`. The external Chainlink price for GOOGLx remained accurate throughout; the vulnerability was entirely in the internal wrapper-to-underlying conversion rate, which was derived from a spot-priced, flash-loan-manipulable source rather than a TWAP or on-chain accumulator. An attacker used a flash loan to perform repeated supply/redeem cycles against the underlying ERC-4626-style vault, pushing the wGOOGLx/GOOGLx rate to roughly 78x its correct value, then borrowed real assets against the phantom-inflated collateral). This is distinct from Pattern 4 (a static governance misconfiguration omitting a price leg, requiring no attacker action) and from Pattern 2 (donation-manipulable raw balance accounting) — Edel's mechanism is an actively attacker-manipulated, spot-derived *conversion rate* between a wrapper token and its underlying asset, with the external price feed itself never compromised. Currently a single incident; not yet promotable to a scored pattern under the two-incident rule. Worth monitoring closely given the stated industry trend toward wrapping tokenized real-world assets (equities, RWA fund shares) as DeFi collateral, which the source briefing analyzing this incident explicitly flagged as a recurring exposure across similar wrapper structures (Ondo Finance's ouTokens, other xStocks-based tokens, tokenized fund shares).
- Deprecated market/vault contracts left live and unaudited after being functionally superseded — cross-reference to `yield-aggregator.md` Pattern 1, where this mechanism is already validated by Yearn's December 2025 exploit. One real case now confirms this same mechanism also applies to Lending specifically: Abracadabra Money (October 4, 2025, ~$1.7M), whose CauldronV4 markets had already been exploited twice before for unrelated reasons ($6.49M, January 2024 — a debt-accounting `base`/`elastic` desync bug, correctly out of scope as a pure code-logic issue per the note above; $13M, March 2025 — a separate GMX V2 integration flaw). The October incident hit a specific, older CauldronV4 deployment that had fallen out of active use, remained technically live and callable, and had not been audited since November 2023 — over 22 months — while development attention moved to newer features. A shared `CookStatus` struct bug (an uninitialized branch silently reset a solvency-check flag) let the attacker borrow without posting real collateral, specifically because this legacy market was no longer under active review. This is the same underlying failure as Yearn's case — a superseded component remaining a live, unmonitored attack surface — just triggered by a code-logic bug rather than a governance-execution failure. Given this is a second real case of the same core mechanism (deprecated-but-live code as attack surface), consider this pattern validated across both Lending and Yield Aggregator; check any protocol's changelog or audit history for markets/vaults that have been functionally superseded but not formally deactivated.
